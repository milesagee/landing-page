Trap zone
These are the trap shapes, not the answers. Spot the trap, mark it, push deeper if you want a harder version.
1. Hold parameters. They give you HR and BP that look borderline. They add a second cardiac med (digoxin, calcium channel blocker, ACE inhibitor) in the stem. The trap is which parameter governs and which med pair you should not stack without thinking about conduction.
One level deeper Stack a calcium channel blocker on top. Same patient. What changes about your hold threshold, and what mechanism makes that decision non-optional rather than judgment?
2. Hypoglycemia masking. Diabetic patient on a beta-blocker. The stem lists symptoms. One of the four classic hypoglycemia signs is missing. The trap is which sign survives the beta-blocker and which one does not.
One level deeper Now name the sign that survives, and connect the mechanism to why it survives while the others go quiet. Then ask: what is the one symptom you teach this patient to trust above all others?
3. Selective vs non-selective. Asthma patient. They prescribe a beta-blocker. The trap is not whether to use one. The trap is which one, at what dose, and what happens to that selectivity if the dose climbs.
One level deeper At what dose does cardio-selectivity stop being selective? Which receptor crosses over first, and what symptom is the early warning on assessment?
4. Abrupt discontinuation. Patient missed three days of metoprolol on a trip. They come in with chest pain. The trap is naming the mechanism that turns missed doses into a coronary event, not just labeling it "rebound."
One level deeper Walk the upregulation pathway. What got upregulated during the chronic block, and why does sudden release of that upregulation hit the heart instead of, say, the lungs or the gut?
5. The HF paradox. Heart failure patient gets a beta-blocker added. Intuition says negative inotrope is bad in HF. The trap is the timing distinction between chronic disease-modifying use and acute decompensation, and what that means for whether you give the dose now.
One level deeper Volume overloaded HF patient just got admitted, already on chronic carvedilol. Surgeon-mode says hold it. Cardiologist-mode says do not. Whose framework wins on day one of this admission, and what is the variable that flips your answer?

The thing nurses get wrong
People assume "negative inotrope = bad in HF" and hold beta-blockers on admission for any decompensation. The chronic, disease-modifying use of beta-blockers in HF is one of the cleanest mortality wins in cardiology, and stopping them inappropriately undoes months of titration. The trap is conflating chronic management with acute presentation. Read the chart, read the volume status, talk to the team. Do not act on instinct.
