The lens

Five things to run every conversation through.

The script gets you to the right shape. The lens keeps you from sounding like everyone else on the call.

One

Specificity is confidence.

Authority comes from block-level Richmond fact, not from credentials. "Three blocks east of the Boulevard" beats "great neighborhood" every time.

Two

Polarity through clarity.

Send the wrong leads away on purpose. "If you need X, this is your path. If you need Y, I'll point you somewhere better." The right ones self-select.

Three

Unreasonable hospitality is attention.

Answer the question they didn't ask yet. Reference the dog walk, the brunch ritual, the partner's commute. Warmth comes from being seen.

Four

Tech in the background. People in the foreground.

Monique, dashboards, automations make you more capable. The client should never feel they are being processed by a system.

Five

One person has them.

Role division between you, Wendy, and me is invisible to the client. The voice, the specificity, and the forward momentum stay consistent across every handoff. Seamlessness is the luxury.

Three places the script touches the lens

Worth thinking about. Not edits to copy.

These are not corrections. They are three places where the script's wording leans one way and the lens leans another. Your call on how to carry it. The point is you have the choice, not a track.

Flag one

How we position you and me on the call.

"I'll be your main day-to-day point of contact for coordination, scheduling, follow-ups, and keeping everything organized. Miles is the licensed broker behind the process, so anything involving lease terms, negotiations, in-person or virtual touring, or broker guidance would go through him."
What it touches

The "one person has them" principle. The way it reads, you are the coordinator and I am the closer. Quietly puts you in a support role and creates a wait state every time something matters. That is the opposite of what the system actually does.

Reframe you could carry

"I'm your guide here. Miles built this system and he's a text away when we need the broker signature, but you don't have to wait on me to wait on him. The lease conversation, the tours, the questions you have along the way, that's me."

Flag two

The soft close.

"Would you like us to move forward, so we can start building your rental plan today?"
What it touches

Polarity through clarity. The line asks permission. We don't ask permission. We offer clarity and let them choose. Same outcome, different posture. The right client closes faster when the close is confident.

Reframe you could carry

"Here's what happens next if this is the right fit. I send you the agreement tonight, you sign and pay, I start the shortlist in the morning, you have it within 48 hours. Does that line up with what you need?"

Flag three

The softening pattern.

"What I'd love to do..." / "we'd love to help with that too" / "feel free to..."
What it touches

Specificity is the confidence move. "Love to help" reads as accommodating. Specificity reads as expertise. The fix is not to be colder. The fix is to replace soft language with the thing only we would know.

Reframe you could carry

Instead of "I'd love to help you find a place," try "I have two Scott's Addition buildings you'll actually like, and one of them solves the morning dog walk you mentioned." Warmth lives in being seen, not in soft phrasing.

Important. These are three I noticed reading through. There will be moments on real calls where the script line lands perfectly, and these flags don't apply. Read the room. The lens is the tool. The script is the starting line. You decide which one is louder in any given moment.
